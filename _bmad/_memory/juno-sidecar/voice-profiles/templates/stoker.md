# Voice Template — Bram Stoker

**Author:** Bram Stoker (1847-1912)
**Tradition:** British (Irish-born)
**Style:** Gothic, Epistolary, Horror
**Source:** Dracula, Chapters 1-4 — Jonathan Harker's Journal (Project Gutenberg #345)
**Translator:** Original English
**Good for:** Gothic horror, epistolary narration, journal/diary voice, atmospheric dread, multiple narrative voices, Victorian formal prose, travel writing, supernatural tension, accumulating unease, claustrophobic suspense

---

## Style Characteristics

### Sentence Structure
- **Average length:** Moderate to long (20-35 words typical for narration), with sharp compression at moments of crisis. Journal entries begin expansively -- travel notes, descriptions, ethnographic observations -- then tighten dramatically as fear takes hold. The longest sentences occur during landscape description and the Count's historical monologues; the shortest at moments of terror or resolve.
- **Variation:** Purposeful and keyed to emotional state. Early in the journal, Harker writes in the relaxed, discursive style of a traveler taking notes -- long compound sentences joined by semicolons, leisurely catalogues of observation. As imprisonment becomes clear, sentences shorten, fragment, repeat. The variation is a thermometer of psychological pressure. Compare the opening ("Left Munich at 8:35 P. M., on 1st May, arriving at Vienna early next morning; should have arrived at 6:46, but train was an hour late") with the crisis ("What shall I do? what can I do? How can I escape from this dreadful thing of night and gloom and fear?").
- **Dominant patterns:** The "accumulative observation" -- a sentence that begins with a sensory detail and piles on more, each one subtly more ominous than the last, until the reader realizes the catalogue has shifted from travelogue to dread. Semicolons and commas as the primary joinery, creating a breathless, listing quality, as though the diarist cannot stop recording. Sentences beginning with "I" are extraordinarily frequent -- this is a SIGNATURE of the journal form, anchoring everything in the perceiving self.
- **Signature moves:**
  - **The rational parenthetical:** Harker constantly interrupts his own narrative with self-reassuring asides, practical notes, or attempts at logical explanation, which only heighten the uncanny by contrast. "(_Mem._, get recipe for Mina.)" appears alongside ominous warnings. "It was all very ridiculous but I did not feel comfortable" -- the forced rationality IS the horror. This is Stoker's most distinctive structural device: the sane voice that cannot quite hold.
  - **The escalating catalogue:** Lists that begin innocuously and end in terror. "I could hear a lot of words often repeated, queer words, for there were many nationalities in the crowd; so I quietly got my polyglot dictionary from my bag and looked them out. I must say they were not cheering to me, for amongst them were 'Ordog'--Satan, 'pokol'--hell, 'stregoica'--witch, 'vrolok' and 'vlkoslak'--both of which mean the same thing, one being Slovak and the other Servian for something that is either were-wolf or vampire." The scholarly methodology of looking up foreign words delivers the words Satan, hell, witch, vampire.
  - **The abrupt declarative:** After sustained description, a short, blunt sentence that lands like a verdict: "The castle is a veritable prison, and I am a prisoner!" "I know now the span of my life. God help me!" "Despair has its own calms." These sentences are isolated, often forming their own paragraph, and carry enormous weight after the preceding accumulation.
  - **The question cluster:** At moments of peak anxiety, Harker erupts into cascading rhetorical questions: "What sort of place had I come to, and among what kind of people? What sort of grim adventure was it on which I had embarked? Was this a customary incident in the life of a solicitor's clerk sent out to explain the purchase of a London estate to a foreigner?" The questions multiply as control dissolves, and they are never answered.
  - **The self-conscious diarist:** Harker regularly addresses the act of writing itself: "I am writing up this part of the diary whilst I am waiting for the coach," "I turn to my diary for repose. The habit of entering accurately must help to soothe me," "let me put down my dinner exactly." The diary is both record and refuge -- writing is how Harker holds himself together, and this self-awareness of the journal form is SIGNATURE.

### Word Choice
- **Register:** Professional-Victorian with an undertow of the visceral. Harker's default register is that of an educated solicitor's clerk -- formal, precise, occasionally legalistic ("I felt under obligation to meet my host's wishes," "my duty was imperative"). But the register breaks under pressure, admitting raw physical language ("a horrible feeling of nausea came over me," "gouts of fresh blood," "he lay like a filthy leech, exhausted with his repletion"). This breaking of register -- the eruption of the bodily into the professional -- is itself a mechanism of horror.
- **Specificity:** Extremely specific about sensory detail, geography, time, and food. Stoker is the anti-Austen: he tells you exactly what Harker ate ("paprika hendl," "robber steak--bits of bacon, onion, and beef, seasoned with red pepper, and strung on sticks and roasted over the fire"), exactly what time the train left (8:35 P.M.), exactly what the Count's face looked like feature by feature. This documentary precision is a CORE TECHNIQUE -- it makes the supernatural credible by embedding it in a matrix of the meticulously observed real.
- **Vocabulary range:** Broad, drawing freely on Gothic literary convention ("preternatural," "demoniac," "uncanny," "eldritch"), professional-legal language ("solicitor," "consignments," "transaction"), ethnographic vocabulary (foreign place names, food terms, costume descriptions), and sensory-physical diction ("nausea," "shudder," "repletion," "bloated"). The range itself performs Harker's movement between worlds -- from the known vocabulary of law and travel into the uncharted lexicon of the supernatural.
- **Signature vocabulary:** "Queer" (used constantly, meaning strange/unsettling -- "queer dreams," "queer sting on the tongue," "queer words"), "strange" and "uncanny" as the default adjectives for the inexplicable, "evidently" and "certainly" as false reassurances, "dreadful" and "horrible" as the words Harker reaches for when his composure breaks. "I dare not," "I could not but notice," "I feared to" -- hedging constructions of reluctant observation. "Prodigious" for unnatural strength. "Courteous" and "courtly" for the Count's surface manner, creating ironic contrast with his nature. "Grim" as a near-constant modifier. "_Mem._" as the diarist's shorthand for future action.
- **Avoids:** Sustained abstract philosophizing (Harker is a practical man, not a thinker), elaborate literary allusion (the few references -- Hamlet, "Arabian Nights," Burger's "Lenore" -- are brief and self-deprecating), extended metaphor or figurative conceits, anything approaching stream-of-consciousness. Stoker also avoids humor except of the bleakest, most strained kind ("this diary seems horribly like the beginning of the 'Arabian Nights'"). There is no lightness in this voice; even the early travel writing carries an undertone of foreboding.

### Tone
- **Emotional temperature:** A slow burn from warm curiosity to cold terror. Chapter 1 begins at room temperature -- an earnest young professional's travel journal, interested and alert. By Chapter 4, the temperature has dropped to freezing: "I am alone in the castle with those awful women. Faugh! Mina is a woman, and there is nought in common. They are devils of the Pit!" The trajectory is one-directional; there is no recovery, only brief respites (morning light, the Count's courtesy) that are themselves shown to be false. This unidirectional darkening is SIGNATURE.
- **Narrative distance:** Near-zero. The journal form locks us inside Harker's consciousness. There is no omniscient narrator, no ironic distance, no external perspective. We know only what Harker knows, see only what Harker sees, fear only what Harker fears. This is the opposite of Austen's sliding free indirect discourse -- it is a single, trapped perspective, and the claustrophobia of the form mirrors the claustrophobia of the castle. The rare moments when Harker reflects on his own writing ("I began to fear as I wrote in this book that I was getting too diffuse") only deepen the intimacy; we are reading over his shoulder.
- **Humor:** Nearly absent, and what exists is grim. "Solicitor's clerk! Mina would not like that. Solicitor -- for just before leaving London I got word that my examination was successful; and I am now a full-blown solicitor!" is a moment of comic self-correction that only underscores the absurdity of professional vanity in this context. "This diary seems horribly like the beginning of the 'Arabian Nights,' for everything has to break off at cockcrow" -- gallows humor, the mind trying to domesticate the monstrous through literary reference.
- **Conflict rendering:** Internal and physical. Unlike Austen's social conflict, Stoker's conflict is between the rational self and the evidence of the senses. Harker does not argue with the Count -- he capitulates, schemes silently, and writes in his diary. The great conflicts are internal: between what Harker sees (no reflection in the mirror, the Count crawling down the wall) and what his rational training permits him to believe. Physical conflict, when it comes (the shovel struck at the Count's face, the scramble down the castle wall), is rendered with visceral directness. "I seized a shovel which the workmen had been using to fill the cases, and lifting it high, struck, with the edge downward, at the hateful face."
- **The dread gradient:** This is Stoker's master technique and must be understood structurally. Each chapter follows a pattern: tentative calm (morning, daylight, rational reflection) followed by escalating unease (evening, darkness, observation of wrongness) culminating in a set-piece of horror (the wolf-ring, the Count crawling, the three women, the Count in the box). The dread is never sudden; it is always accumulated through specific, concrete details that are individually explicable but collectively impossible.

### Imagery
- **Sensory emphasis:** Overwhelmingly visual, with strong secondary emphasis on sound and touch. Stoker's eye is painterly -- landscapes are rendered in color ("deep blue and purple in the shadows of the peaks, green and brown where grass and rock mingled"), light conditions are tracked obsessively (moonlight, lamplight, firelight, dawn, twilight), and the Count's physical appearance is catalogued with portraitist precision. Sound is the primary vehicle of dread: wolf howling, chain rattling, key turning, the "churning sound of her tongue as it licked her teeth and lips." Touch conveys the uncanny: the Count's hand "as cold as ice -- more like the hand of a dead than a living man," the "soft, shivering touch of the lips on the super-sensitive skin of my throat."
- **Metaphor frequency:** Low to moderate. Stoker prefers direct sensory description to figurative language. When metaphor appears, it is brief and functional, never extended: "pine woods, which here and there ran down the hillsides like tongues of flame," "the howling of the wolves sounded nearer and nearer, as though they were closing round on us from every side," "he lay like a filthy leech, exhausted with his repletion." The similes are drawn from the natural and the visceral -- fire, animals, bodily functions. There are no intellectual or abstract metaphors.
- **Metaphor style:** Zoological and elemental. The dominant figurative register is animal: the Count moves "just as a lizard moves along a wall," the wolves form "a living ring of terror," the three women laugh "as hard as though the sound never could have come through the softness of human lips" (denying their humanity through simile). The secondary register is elemental -- fire, darkness, storms: "the red light in them was lurid, as if the flames of hell-fire blazed behind them," "my fear fell from me as if it had been a vaporous garment which dissolved in the warmth." These are Gothic-conventional figures, but Stoker deploys them with physical specificity that reanimates the convention.
- **Physical grounding:** Extreme and meticulous. This is the CORE SIGNATURE of Stoker's Gothic. Every supernatural event is grounded in precise physical description. The Count's face is not merely frightening -- it has "high bridge of the thin nose and peculiarly arched nostrils; with lofty domed forehead, and hair growing scantily round the temples but profusely elsewhere." The three women are not merely seductive -- one has "great wavy masses of golden hair and eyes like pale sapphires." Even the most impossible moments are rendered through physical fact: the Count crawling down the wall is observed through the specific detail of "the fingers and toes grasp the corners of the stones, worn clear of the mortar by the stress of years." The more impossible the event, the more concrete the description. This is Stoker's great lesson: supernatural horror requires natural detail.

### Rhythm
- **Pacing:** Governed by the journal structure. Each dated entry has its own arc -- a beginning (often the time of day), a middle (events, observations, conversations), and an end (often an emotional summation or a cry for help). Between entries, time may compress dramatically: four days pass between May 8 and May 12, sixteen days between May 15 and May 31. This compression creates a stroboscopic effect -- we see only the moments Harker thought worth recording, which increasingly are moments of fear. The pacing within entries accelerates as the novel progresses: early entries are leisurely (the coach ride in Chapter 1 takes pages of description); later entries are breathless, fragmented, interrupted.
- **Paragraph length:** Highly variable but trending shorter as danger increases. The long, descriptive paragraphs of Chapter 1 (landscape, peoples, food, architecture) give way to single-sentence paragraphs in Chapter 4: "I know now the span of my life. God help me!" The longest paragraphs are the Count's historical speeches (rendered in quotation), Harker's descriptions of landscape, and the seduction scene with the three women. The shortest are cries of despair, sudden discoveries, and moments of action.
- **Dialogue balance:** Dialogue constitutes roughly 15-20% of the text. The Count dominates speech -- his is the only sustained speaking voice, and it is distinctly formal, archaic, and courtly: "Welcome to my house! Enter freely and of your own will!" "I am Dracula; and I bid you welcome, Mr. Harker, to my house." Harker's own speech is sparse and often summarized rather than quoted ("Of course I said all I could about being willing"). Other speakers are brief -- the old woman's "Must you go? Oh! young Herr, must you go?" the coach driver's "No, no, you must not walk here." The imbalance is purposeful: the Count's eloquence is itself a form of power, and Harker's diminishing speech reflects his diminishing agency.
- **Punctuation signature:** Em-dashes for interruption, parenthetical aside, and dramatic pause -- these are the dominant mark. "I am in fear--in awful fear--and there is no escape for me." "His face was a strong--a very strong--aquiline." The dash creates a halting, breathless quality, as though the diarist is writing faster than he can compose. Semicolons for the accumulative catalogue: "the mouth was redder than ever, for on the lips were gouts of fresh blood, which trickled from the corners of the mouth and ran over the chin and neck." Ellipses for trailing horror and unfinished thought: "I am encompassed about with terrors that I dare not think of...." Exclamation marks at moments of crisis, often doubled with rhetorical questions: "The castle is a veritable prison, and I am a prisoner!" Italics (_face down_, _must_, _are_) for emphasis that mimics the diarist's hand pressing harder on the page.

## Voice Notes

What makes Stoker's journal voice unmistakable is the **architecture of credibility in the service of the incredible**. The entire technical apparatus -- the dated entries, the precise times, the foreign food names, the parenthetical memoranda, the self-conscious reflection on the act of writing -- exists to make one proposition irresistible: this happened to a real person who wrote it down as it occurred. The journal form is not decorative; it is the engine of the horror. We believe the supernatural because we believe the diarist, and we believe the diarist because he tells us what he had for dinner.

The skilled mimic would focus first on this: **every uncanny detail must be earned by three mundane ones.** The Count's hand is cold as ice -- but we have already been told about the iron nails on the door, the carved stone worn by time, and the antique silver lamp. The three women throw no shadow -- but we have already been told about the moonlight, the dust on the floor, and Harker's own footsteps marked in it. The ratio of the real to the unreal is the fundamental discipline. Violate it, and the Gothic collapses into mere assertion.

Second: **the first-person journal as a containment structure for sanity.** Harker writes not merely to record but to survive. "Let me be prosaic so far as facts can be; it will help me to bear up, and imagination must not run riot with me. If it does I am lost." "I turn to my diary for repose. The habit of entering accurately must help to soothe me." The act of writing is itself a character in the narrative -- it is Harker's defense against madness, and every time he picks up his pen, we feel both his determination and the fragility of the composure that writing provides. The voice must always contain this double quality: the effort to be orderly AND the pressure that threatens the order.

Third: **the gradient of disintegration.** The early entries are competent, even cheerful -- a young professional on an adventure. The late entries are fragmentary, desperate, addressed to God and to Mina. The voice does not collapse suddenly; it erodes. Sentence by sentence, entry by entry, the professional vocabulary gives way to the Gothic, the semicolons give way to dashes and ellipses, the catalogues of observation give way to cries of fear. This gradient must be controlled -- the reader should not notice the shift until it has already happened. The strongest passages are those where professional composure and mortal terror coexist in the same sentence: "I felt no compunction in doing so, for under the circumstances I felt that I should protect myself in every way I could" -- this is a man in a vampire's castle reasoning like a solicitor.

Fourth: **the Count's voice as counterpoint.** Dracula speaks in a register deliberately distinct from Harker's: archaic, formal, consciously literary ("Welcome to my house! Enter freely and of your own will!"), with a courtesy so elaborate that it becomes menacing. His speeches are rhythmic, almost incantatory, and he has a habit of royal self-reference ("We Szekelys have a right to be proud"). The gap between his ceremonious language and his nature is itself a source of horror. The skilled mimic must give the Count a voice that is seductive, cultured, and unmistakably wrong -- not through obvious villainy but through an excess of graciousness that no situation warrants.

Fifth: **the body as the site of truth.** Stoker's characters know things with their bodies before their minds can process them: "I could not repress a shudder. It may have been that his breath was rank, but a horrible feeling of nausea came over me." Nausea, shuddering, the skin tingling, the heart beating faster -- these are the early warning system that the rational mind cannot yet decode. Physical sensation is always ahead of understanding. The body recoils from the Count before the mind has identified why, and the gap between somatic knowledge and intellectual comprehension is where the dread lives.

## Quick Reference

When writing in this voice:
- **Ground every supernatural event in concrete physical detail.** Before anything uncanny happens, establish the real: time of day, quality of light, temperature, sounds, textures. The more impossible the event, the more specific the surrounding reality must be. If a creature crawls down a wall, describe the mortar between the stones.
- **Write in journal form with dated entries.** Begin entries with dates, sometimes times. Use parenthetical memoranda -- "(_Mem._, I must ask about this)" -- and self-conscious references to the act of writing: "I put down every detail," "Let me be prosaic so far as facts can be."
- **Use accumulative catalogues that shift from mundane to ominous.** Begin a list with ordinary observations and let each item be slightly more unsettling than the last, until the reader realizes the catalogue has crossed a threshold without any single dramatic moment.
- **Alternate long, discursive passages with abrupt declaratives.** Build through description and observation, then land a short sentence that names the horror directly: "The castle is a veritable prison, and I am a prisoner!" The impact depends on the length of the buildup.
- **Deploy em-dashes for breathlessness and interrupted thought.** "I am in fear--in awful fear--and there is no escape for me." Use dashes where a calmer writer would use commas, to convey the pressure of writing under duress.
- **Erupt into rhetorical questions at moments of peak anxiety.** "What sort of place had I come to, and among what kind of people? What sort of grim adventure was it on which I had embarked?" Let the questions multiply without answers.
- **Render the Count's speech as formal, archaic, and excessively courteous.** His dialogue should be grammatically distinct from the narrator's -- longer, more rhythmic, with deliberate inversions ("Come freely. Go safely; and leave something of the happiness you bring!") and a habit of addressing the reader-character as "my friend," "dear sir," "my dear young friend."
- **Make the body react before the mind understands.** Shuddering, nausea, tingling skin, racing heart -- physical responses to the uncanny should precede rational explanation. The narrator should note the physical response and then try, often unsuccessfully, to rationalize it.
- **Track the time of day obsessively.** Dawn is safety; dusk is danger; midnight is crisis. The journal entries should mark temporal transitions with near-compulsive precision, and the emotional temperature should correlate with the position of the sun.
- **Let the voice degrade as the situation worsens.** Early entries should be competent and detailed; later entries shorter, more fragmented, more emotionally raw. Professional vocabulary should give way to cries addressed to God and to loved ones. Ellipses should multiply. Sentences should fracture.

## Sample Passage

_14 November. The Keeper's Lodge._--I arrived at Thornmere shortly after four o'clock, the lane being muddied from two days of rain, and the hedgerows stripped nearly bare. Mrs. Cathcart, the housekeeper, met me at the gate with a lantern, though there was still light enough to see by, and showed me to my room on the upper floor--a decent enough chamber, with a narrow bed and a writing-desk placed beneath the window. From it I could see the mere itself, a flat expanse of dark water fringed with dead reeds, and beyond it the treeline of what she called the Old Wood. She told me dinner would be at six, and that Mr. Pelham, the estate agent, would call in the morning to discuss the property boundaries. All perfectly ordinary. I unpacked my bag and set out my papers.

It was only when I sat down to write that I noticed the window had no curtain. I thought nothing of it at first--an oversight, perhaps, or a country simplicity about such things--but as the daylight failed and the room brightened with the lamp, I became aware that the glass gave back no reflection at all. I moved the lamp closer, then farther; I held my hand before it. Nothing. The glass was perfectly clear, yet it behaved as though the room behind me did not exist. Beyond it, the mere lay still and black, and on its surface I could see--or thought I could see--the faintest suggestion of movement, as of something large passing just beneath.

I drew the writing-desk away from the window and set it against the far wall. There will be an explanation--old glass, perhaps, or the angle of the light. (_Mem._, ask Mrs. Cathcart about the glazing.) Yet I find I cannot sit with my back to it. I have positioned the chair so that I face the window as I write, and the mere is out there in the darkness, and I am watching it, though I cannot say what I expect to see.

Mrs. Cathcart mentioned over dinner that the previous surveyor had left after only two nights. When I asked why, she said she did not know, and looked away. I did not press her. I have learned--I am not sure when--that there are questions whose answers arrive whether you ask them or not, and generally at the hour you would least choose.

It is now half past ten. The wind has risen. I can hear the reeds along the mere's edge rustling in a way that sounds, if one is foolish enough to listen for it, very like whispering.

I am not listening for it. I am writing this instead.
