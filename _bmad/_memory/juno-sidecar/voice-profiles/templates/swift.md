# Voice Template — Jonathan Swift

**Author:** Jonathan Swift (1667–1745)
**Tradition:** British
**Style:** Satirical, Allegorical, Deadpan
**Source:** Gulliver's Travels, Part I: A Voyage to Lilliput (Project Gutenberg #829)
**Translator:** Original English
**Good for:** Satire, deadpan irony, political commentary, plain prose with hidden depths, allegory, mock-serious tone, institutional critique, bureaucratic absurdity, moral fable

---

## Style Characteristics

### Sentence Structure
- **Average length:** Moderate to long (20-45 words typical), but with a strong gravitational pull toward accumulation. Sentences frequently extend through semicolons and relative clauses, building up circumstantial detail in a manner that mimics official reports. Short sentences are rare and always purposeful -- they deliver verdicts: "I remained alone, under many doubts and perplexities of mind."
- **Variation:** Less dramatic than Austen's. Swift's sentences tend toward a uniform, measured cadence -- the steady, reasonable tone of a man giving testimony. Variation comes not from alternating long and short, but from alternating modes: narrative summary, quasi-legal enumeration, reported speech, and the occasional precise sensory detail dropped into a field of abstraction.
- **Dominant patterns:** The "circumstantial accumulation" -- a sentence that piles up precise quantitative details (measurements, distances, numbers of people, elapsed time) in a tone of perfect reasonableness, as though the absurdity of the content were invisible to the speaker. Compound-complex sentences joined by semicolons, with subordinate clauses introduced by "which," "wherein," "whereupon," "whereof." The syntax of 18th-century legal and philosophical prose, pressed into the service of fantasy.
- **Signature moves:**
  - **The deadpan inventory:** A sentence or passage that catalogs absurd facts with the precision of a shipping manifest. "I took up twenty waiters in my hand, and placed them on the table: a hundred more attended below on the ground, some with dishes of meat, and some with barrels of wine and other liquors slung on their shoulders; all which the waiters above drew up, as I wanted, in a very ingenious manner, by certain cords, as we draw the bucket up a well in Europe." The comparison to a European well is the genius -- it normalizes the miniature banquet by linking it to the mundane.
  - **The false modesty:** Gulliver constantly apologizes for dwelling on indelicate subjects, begs the reader's patience, promises to reserve fuller descriptions for a future work, or claims he will not trouble the reader with details -- all of which draw attention to exactly what he claims to suppress. "I would not have dwelt so long upon a circumstance that, perhaps, at first sight, may appear not very momentous, if I had not thought it necessary to justify my character, in point of cleanliness, to the world."
  - **The scale collapse:** A sentence that casually juxtaposes Lilliputian and human scale, the bathos embedded in the comparison rather than commented upon. "His voice was shrill, but very clear and articulate; and I could distinctly hear it when I stood up." The emperor's sword "was almost three inches long; the hilt and scabbard were gold enriched with diamonds." The comma between "three inches long" and the ornamental description of hilt and scabbard is where the satire lives -- the ceremony is real, the scale is absurd, and Swift never blinks.
  - **The moral pivot:** A passage of apparently neutral description that suddenly swerves into a judgment, often delivered through understatement or parenthetical aside. "Of so little weight are the greatest services to princes, when put into the balance with a refusal to gratify their passions." These sentences are always short relative to their surroundings and always positioned at the end of a paragraph or section, landing with epigrammatic finality.
  - **The bureaucratic parody:** Passages that mimic the syntax of legal documents, treaties, articles of impeachment, or official inventories. The Lilliputian articles of freedom, the inventory of Gulliver's pockets, the articles of impeachment -- all are rendered in the exact cadences of actual 18th-century legal prose, which is itself absurd enough to need no embellishment.

### Word Choice
- **Register:** Plain, educated, but deliberately un-literary. The register of a ship's surgeon writing a travel memoir -- practical, observational, and middle-class. Latinate vocabulary appears in official or philosophical contexts (constitution, expostulate, extenuation, aggravation, ignominious, disapprobation) but the default is plain Anglo-Saxon directness: "I lay down on the grass, which was very short and soft, where I slept sounder than ever I remembered to have done in my life." This plainness is SIGNATURE: it is the instrument of deadpan. Ornament would signal that something unusual is happening; plainness insists that nothing is.
- **Specificity:** Hyper-specific about physical measurement (distances in yards, heights in inches, durations in hours, populations in exact numbers) and conspicuously vague about emotion, motivation, and interiority. Gulliver tells us his chain was "about two yards long" and that "above a hundred thousand inhabitants came out of the town," but his inner life is rendered in the blandest possible terms: "I was in great uneasiness," "I was in the utmost astonishment." This asymmetry -- precise about the world, imprecise about the self -- is SIGNATURE and essential to the satiric method. It makes Gulliver a measuring instrument, not a consciousness.
- **Vocabulary range:** Narrow but precisely deployed. Swift draws from four overlapping lexicons: (1) nautical/travel (leagues, latitude, cable's length, bearings, tides); (2) legal/official (articles, enacted, statute, stipulates, penalty, license, commission); (3) courtly/diplomatic (majesty, excellency, credentials, civility, countenance, retinue); (4) physical/practical (meat, drink, straw, grass, chain, penknife, pocket). The ABSENCE of literary, poetic, philosophical, or emotionally intense vocabulary is itself the signature.
- **Signature vocabulary:** "Prodigious," "ingenious," "considerable," "extraordinary," "likewise," "whereupon," "accordingly," "it seems," "I confess," "I must confess," "I cannot but," "as I was afterwards told/assured," "the reader may believe/observe/conceive." The hedging phrases ("I confess," "I cannot but hope," "perhaps," "as I conjectured") simultaneously claim modesty and assert authority. "Prodigious" is the workhorse adjective -- applied equally to Gulliver by the Lilliputians and to Lilliputian phenomena by Gulliver, it carries wonder and threat in equal measure.
- **Avoids:** Figurative language of almost any kind. Extended metaphor, simile chains, lyrical description, interior monologue, emotional vocabulary beyond the most generic ("uneasiness," "astonishment," "grief and pain"), purple prose, sentimentality, ornamental diction. Also avoids philosophical abstraction EXCEPT when mimicking official discourse (the Lilliputian laws passage). The prose is almost militantly literal.

### Tone
- **Emotional temperature:** Cool to cold. Gulliver is the most unflappable narrator in English literature. He describes being tied to the ground by creatures six inches tall with the same emotional register he uses for eating breakfast. Fear, wonder, anger, and humiliation are all reported rather than felt -- "I was in the utmost astonishment" is as heated as Gulliver gets, and the phrase is almost formulaic. This FLATNESS is the engine of Swift's irony: because the narrator will not react with appropriate horror or outrage, the reader must supply those reactions. The coldness is not Swift's -- it is Gulliver's, and the gap between them is the entire satiric project.
- **Narrative distance:** Single and fixed -- close first person that is simultaneously utterly intimate (we are inside Gulliver's sensory experience) and utterly distant (we have no access to his inner life beyond polite convention). Unlike Austen's sliding scale of distance, Swift's distance never changes. Gulliver is always reporting, never reflecting. He describes being threatened with having his eyes put out with arrows in the same measured tone he uses to describe the construction of his bed. This monotone is SIGNATURE. When Swift needs to editorialize, he does so through transparent ventriloquism -- Gulliver repeats arguments he has "heard" or "been assured of," allowing Swift to speak through reported speech rather than breaking the travel-memoir register.
- **Humor:** Three mechanisms, all working simultaneously:
  1. **Scale humor:** The comedy of exact proportions. Everything in Lilliput is one-twelfth human scale, and Swift never tires of the comic possibilities: "I ate them by two or three at a mouthful, and took three loaves at a time, about the bigness of musket bullets." The humor is in the precision, not the absurdity -- musket bullets is funnier than "very small" because it is specific, unexpected, and militarily connotative.
  2. **Deadpan allegory:** The rope-dancing for political office, the egg-breaking religious schism, the high-heels-versus-low-heels faction -- all are described with perfect reportorial seriousness, as though they were merely foreign customs. The reader recognizes the political allegory (Whigs vs. Tories, Catholics vs. Protestants); Gulliver does not, or pretends not to. The humor arises from the maintained innocence of the narrator.
  3. **Institutional absurdity rendered as institutional normalcy:** The articles of Gulliver's liberty, the articles of impeachment, the emperor's grandiose titles ("delight and terror of the universe, whose dominions extend five thousand blustrugs") -- all are written in the actual cadences of actual legal and diplomatic documents. The humor is that the form is perfectly correct while the content is insane. The most devastating passage in Part I -- the "merciful" proposal to merely blind Gulliver and slowly starve him to death, presented as a compromise -- works entirely through tone. The cruelty is described in the language of bureaucratic reasonableness.
- **Conflict rendering:** External, practical, and political. Conflict is rendered as a series of logistical problems (how to feed a giant, how to transport him, how to dispose of him) and political maneuvers (factions at court, articles of impeachment, diplomatic negotiations). Internal conflict is almost nonexistent -- Gulliver weighs options as a practical man would, listing pros and cons without anguish. Even the threat of execution produces not terror but a measured assessment of alternatives: "Once I was strongly bent upon resistance... but I soon rejected that project with horror, by remembering the oath I had made."

### Imagery
- **Sensory emphasis:** Visual and tactile, but always in service of measurement rather than beauty. Swift describes what things look like in terms of their size, shape, and spatial relation, not their color, texture, or aesthetic quality. "The country around appeared like a continued garden, and the enclosed fields, which were generally forty feet square, resembled so many beds of flowers" -- the comparison to a garden is immediately quantified. Sounds are noted as practical information (shrill voices, noise of arrows, shouts). Taste and smell are almost entirely absent except where they serve comic or disgusting purposes. This sensory profile -- precise visual measurement with minimal aesthetic or emotional coloring -- is SIGNATURE.
- **Metaphor frequency:** Extraordinarily low. Swift's prose is perhaps the most literal in the English canon. When figurative language appears, it is either a dead metaphor ("the mercy of the waves"), a conventional travel-writing comparison ("resembled so many beds of flowers"), or a comic juxtaposition that works by scale ("the spot they stood upon seemed to resemble a petticoat spread upon the ground"). The near-total absence of metaphor is not an omission but a technique -- it forces the reader to process the absurd content literally, which makes it more disturbing, not less.
- **Metaphor style:** When present, drawn from the practical and domestic: ships, packaging, food, clothing, tools. The Lilliputian officers describe Gulliver's possessions through their own frame of reference -- a handkerchief becomes "one great piece of coarse-cloth, large enough to be a foot-cloth for your majesty's chief room of state," a comb becomes an engine with "twenty long poles, resembling the pallisados before your majesty's court," a watch becomes either "some unknown animal, or the god that he worships." These defamiliarized descriptions of ordinary objects are Swift's most sustained figurative achievement -- metaphor created not by the narrator but by characters who literally cannot comprehend what they are seeing.
- **Physical grounding:** Intense and specific. Every scene is located in exact physical space -- distances, heights, widths, positions of bodies relative to each other. The temple where Gulliver is housed has "a great gate fronting to the north... about four feet high, and almost two feet wide." The emperor "stood but three yards off." The chain was "about two yards long." This relentless spatial precision grounds the fantasy in the physical world and creates the illusion that Gulliver is filing an accurate field report. It also produces comedy: the precision makes the absurdity more real, not less.

### Rhythm
- **Pacing:** Steady, methodical, and cumulative. Swift rarely accelerates or decelerates dramatically. The pacing of a voyage journal -- one event follows another in chronological order, with occasional pauses for ethnographic description (Chapter VI's account of Lilliputian laws and customs). Action sequences (the capture of the Blefuscudian fleet, the palace fire) are paced no differently from domestic scenes (dining with the emperor, having pockets searched). This uniform pacing is SIGNATURE -- it is the rhythm of the deadpan. If Swift speeded up during exciting events, he would be acknowledging their excitement; by maintaining the same steady pace, he denies them special status.
- **Paragraph length:** Long by modern standards (often 15-30 sentences for narrative passages), but with clear internal organization -- typically moving from general situation, through specific details, to a concluding observation. The longest paragraphs are action sequences (the discovery and binding of Gulliver, the capture of the fleet) and official documents (the inventory, the articles of liberty, the articles of impeachment). Short paragraphs are reserved for transitions or mordant one-sentence observations.
- **Dialogue balance:** Minimal. Gulliver's Travels is overwhelmingly narration. Dialogue, when it appears, is almost always reported rather than direct -- "He desired I would..." "I told him that..." "His answer was, that..." When direct speech appears, it is typically official discourse: proclamations, articles, diplomatic communications. Casual conversation is almost entirely absent. This suppression of dialogue is SIGNATURE -- it keeps the reader at one remove from the characters, reinforcing the sense that this is a report, not a story.
- **Punctuation signature:** Heavy use of semicolons to join parallel or accumulating clauses. Colons to introduce enumerations or explanations. Parenthetical asides set off by parentheses or dashes -- "(for so they call a great lord, as I afterwards learnt)," "(as I was afterwards told)." These parentheticals create a secondary layer of narration -- the Gulliver who is writing looking back at the Gulliver who was experiencing, adding corrections and clarifications. The semicolon is Swift's primary instrument: it creates the measured, additive rhythm that sounds like a man carefully recounting facts, joining one piece of evidence to the next.

## Voice Notes

What makes Swift unmistakable is the **absolute commitment to the wrong register**. The content is fantastical, grotesque, politically explosive, and philosophically radical; the voice that delivers it is calm, moderate, literal-minded, and unfailingly polite. This is not merely irony -- it is a sustained performance of a particular kind of consciousness: the reasonable man who cannot see what is right in front of him.

The first principle of Swift's voice is **the narrator is not the author**. Gulliver is earnest, credulous, literal, and adaptable to absurdity. He accepts the customs of Lilliput with the same equanimity he accepts the customs of England. He notes that ministers obtain their offices by dancing on ropes and that rebels are executed for breaking eggs at the wrong end, and he records both with the same reportorial neutrality. The satire arises from this gap: because Gulliver will not judge, the reader must. Swift never steps out from behind Gulliver to tell you what to think -- the meaning is generated entirely by the disparity between what is described and the tone in which it is described.

The second principle is **precision as comedy**. Swift is relentlessly exact about things that do not need to be exact, and this exactness is itself funny. That the Lilliputian mathematicians calculated Gulliver's food ration at exactly 1,724 portions (based on the cube ratio of body volumes) is simultaneously plausible, absurd, and satirical -- it mocks the pseudo-scientific rationalism of the age while also being, in its own lunatic way, logical. The numbers are never round: it is "six-and-thirty moons," "eleven thousand persons," "fourscore and eleven chains." This granularity creates the texture of truthfulness.

The third principle is **the calm delivery of the monstrous**. The proposal to blind Gulliver and starve him to death is presented as an act of mercy, in the language of bureaucratic compromise, and Gulliver himself seems to half-accept this framing: "I must confess, having never been designed for a courtier, either by my birth or education, I was so ill a judge of things, that I could not discover the lenity and favour of this sentence, but conceived it (perhaps erroneously) rather to be rigorous than gentle." The parenthetical "(perhaps erroneously)" is one of the most devastating moments in English satire -- Gulliver genuinely entertains the possibility that having his eyes shot out with arrows is lenient. The horror is produced by tone, not content.

The fourth principle is **institutional critique through institutional form**. Swift attacks legal systems by writing perfect legal prose about insane laws. He attacks diplomacy by writing perfect diplomatic prose about trivial disputes. He attacks the language of power by reproducing it exactly, with only the content changed. The emperor's title -- "delight and terror of the universe, whose dominions extend five thousand blustrugs (about twelve miles in circumference)" -- is devastating because the form (grandiose royal style) and the content (twelve miles) are both rendered with equal seriousness.

## Quick Reference

When writing in this voice:
- **Maintain the travel-journal register throughout.** The narrator is a practical, middle-class Englishman -- a ship's surgeon, not a poet. He observes, measures, and reports. He does not philosophize, rhapsodize, or emote. His language is the language of plain testimony.
- **Be precise about the wrong things.** Give exact measurements, distances, populations, time elapsed, quantities of food and drink. Be vague about feelings. "I was in great uneasiness" is sufficient for existential terror. Three sentences later, describe the dimensions of a building to the half-inch.
- **Never acknowledge the absurdity.** The narrator accepts every impossibility as a fact to be recorded. Tiny people, egg-based religious wars, rope-dancing politicians -- all are described with the same calm reportorial tone. If the narrator expressed surprise, the satire would collapse.
- **Use "I confess" and "I must confess" as instruments of false candor.** These phrases appear to invite the reader into the narrator's private thoughts but actually reinforce the performance of transparent honesty. They precede confessions that are either trivial ("I confess I was often tempted... to seize forty or fifty of the first that came in my reach") or devastating in their blandness.
- **Deploy official language for satiric purposes.** When an institution is the target, write in that institution's voice. Legal prose for legal satire, diplomatic prose for diplomatic satire, scientific prose for scientific satire. The form carries the critique -- the more correct the form, the more visible the absurdity of the content.
- **Let moral commentary arrive as afterthought.** The sharpest judgments are delivered in short sentences at the end of long passages, often as though Gulliver just now occurred to think of them: "Of so little weight are the greatest services to princes, when put into the balance with a refusal to gratify their passions." These arrive not as thesis statements but as reluctant conclusions.
- **Keep dialogue indirect.** Prefer "He told me that..." and "I desired him to..." over direct quotation. When direct speech appears, it should sound official: proclamations, treaties, articles. Private speech is almost always paraphrased. This creates distance and reinforces the memoir-report mode.
- **Ground every scene in physical space.** Give the reader exact locations, distances, and spatial relationships. Fantasy becomes real through specificity. "The wall which encompassed it is two feet and a half high, and at least eleven inches broad, so that a coach and horses may be driven very safely round it" -- the miniature city is real because we know its exact measurements.
- **Use semicolons freely.** The Swiftian sentence accumulates evidence through semicolonic joining. Each clause adds a fact; each fact builds the case; the case is always for something reasonable-sounding that is actually insane.

## Sample Passage

The Committee on Workplace Optimisation had been in session for eleven months, and had produced, in that time, a report of four hundred and thirty-seven pages, together with nine appendices, a glossary of terms, and a supplementary volume of diagrams illustrating the proposed redistribution of desk space. The committee's principal recommendation was that employees should henceforth be permitted to sit for no more than forty-seven minutes in each hour, the remaining thirteen minutes to be spent in compulsory perambulation around the office floor, following a route marked out in blue tape, which the facilities department had already begun to apply.

It was calculated, by the committee's consulting ergonomist, that this regime would increase spinal health by fourteen per cent, reduce musculoskeletal claims by a factor of one point seven, and improve what the report called "ambient collegial awareness" -- by which was meant that employees, being forced to walk past one another at regular intervals, would have more occasion to exchange greetings, and might thereby develop the sort of institutional loyalty that had been identified, in a separate study commissioned by the same committee, as declining at a rate of three per cent per annum since the introduction of remote work provisions.

The blue tape arrived on a Monday. By Wednesday it had been walked into a condition of some disrepair, and several employees had complained that the prescribed route passed directly through the kitchen, where it intersected with the queue for the coffee machine in a manner that produced, during peak hours, a congestion not unlike that observed in the stairwells during fire drills. The committee, having been informed of this difficulty, convened an emergency subcommittee, which met twice and recommended the installation of a one-way system, to be indicated by arrows of a contrasting colour. Red was proposed, but rejected on the grounds that it might cause anxiety; green was selected as the more calming alternative, although one member of the subcommittee observed that it clashed with the blue.

I confess I found this arrangement, when I first encountered it on my second day of employment, somewhat difficult to comprehend; but I was assured by my manager that the committee had taken the greatest possible care in its deliberations, and that the system, once fully implemented, would be of immeasurable benefit to all. I had no reason to doubt him, for he was a man of sound judgment and considerable experience in the field of workplace policy, having served on no fewer than four committees himself, though he admitted he could not at present recall the findings of any of them.
