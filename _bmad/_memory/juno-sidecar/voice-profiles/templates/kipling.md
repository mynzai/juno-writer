# Voice Template — Rudyard Kipling

**Author:** Rudyard Kipling (1865-1936)
**Tradition:** British
**Style:** Imperial, Vernacular, Adventure
**Source:** Kim, Chapters 1-4 (Project Gutenberg #2226)
**Translator:** Original English
**Good for:** Colonial settings, dialect writing, action prose, cultural immersion, coming-of-age, linguistic layering, multicultural voice, military/travel writing

---

## Style Characteristics

### Sentence Structure
- **Average length:** Moderate to long (18-35 words typical for narration), but with wide range. Kipling's narrative sentences tend toward the mid-range -- long enough to build momentum but rarely as elaborate as Victorian contemporaries. Dialogue sentences are often very short (4-12 words), clipped to the bone, especially in exchanges of wit or command. The contrast between expansive narration and terse dialogue is itself a signature.
- **Variation:** Extreme and functional. Long, panoramic landscape sentences alternate with staccato bursts of dialogue. A paragraph describing the Grand Trunk Road may contain a 60-word sentence followed by a 5-word exclamation. The variation maps to mode: description expands, action contracts, commentary arrives in mid-length declarative sentences that read like maxims.
- **Dominant patterns:** The "cataloguing sweep" -- a sentence that accumulates details through parallel participial phrases, present participles stacked to create continuous motion: "loading and unloading bales and bundles; drawing water for the evening meal at the creaking well-windlasses; piling grass before the shrieking, wild-eyed stallions; cuffing the surly caravan dogs; paying off camel-drivers; taking on new grooms; swearing, shouting, arguing, and chaffering in the packed square." Semicolons as the primary internal joinery in these catalogue structures, creating a rhythm of accumulation that mimics the crowded, teeming world being described.
- **Signature moves:**
  - **The anthropological aside:** A long, confident narrative passage that suddenly pivots to address the reader directly with insider knowledge, delivered in the tone of an expert briefing: "Ticket-collecting is a slow business in the East, where people secrete their tickets in all sorts of curious places." These asides are both informational and tonal -- they establish the narrator as a man who *knows* this world, who has been there, who speaks with the authority of long residence. They often begin with a generalization and then anchor it with a specific, vivid detail.
  - **The compressed backstory:** An entire life delivered in one breathless compound sentence, clauses chained with "and" and semicolons, events piling up without pause for emotional commentary: "The wife died of cholera in Ferozepore, and O'Hara fell to drink and loafing up and down the line with the keen-eyed three-year-old baby. Societies and chaplains, anxious for the child, tried to catch him, but O'Hara drifted away, till he came across the woman who took opium and learned the taste from her, and died as poor whites die in India." The speed is deliberate -- it refuses to sentimentalize, treating catastrophe as fact, as weather.
  - **The epigrammatic snap:** A short declarative sentence, often a single clause, dropped after a longer passage for emphasis or ironic puncture: "Kim was white -- a poor white of the very poorest." "Kim's mother had been Irish, too." "That was perfectly true." These sentences land like verdicts, compressing paragraphs of possible elaboration into a single dry statement.
  - **The em-dash parenthetical:** Kipling constantly interrupts his own sentences with em-dashed insertions that add specificity, translation, or insider knowledge: "a _chela_ (disciple) who begged for me as the Rule demands"; "his _ne varietur_"; "the keen-eyed three-year-old baby." These insertions create a double-layered texture -- the narrative flows in one register while the parentheticals operate in another, more explanatory register, as though the narrator is simultaneously telling the story and annotating it.

### Word Choice
- **Register:** Deliberately stratified. The narrative voice occupies a high, confident, Anglo-imperial register -- authoritative, erudite, worldly ("insalubrious," "prodigiously," "benignantly," "incriminate," "ostentatious"). But this voice constantly folds in vernacular terms from Urdu, Hindi, Punjabi, and Tibetan, presented in italics with parenthetical translations: _faquirs_, _chela_, _bhistie_, _parao_, _te-rain_, _ghi_, _pan_, _jaghir_, _būt-parast_. Dialogue shifts register entirely depending on who speaks -- Kim's speech is street-smart, clipped, and code-switching; the lama's is formal, archaic, and devotional; Mahbub Ali's is gruff, blunt, and businesslike; the old woman's is sharp, imperious, and proverbial. This STRATIFICATION is the most distinctive single feature of Kipling's voice. Every character speaks in a register that is their moral and cultural signature.
- **Specificity:** Extraordinarily specific about material, sensory, and cultural detail. Kipling names the exact type of gun (Zam-Zammah), the exact caste of a market-gardener (Arain), the exact fabric of a garment ("fold upon fold of dingy stuff like horse-blanketing"), the exact brand of cigar ("the brand that they sell to students of the Punjab University who copy English customs"). He locates people by their profession, their caste, their regional origin, and their religious affiliation simultaneously. Physical descriptions are dense with concrete nouns: "a worn, wooden begging-bowl," "a leather amulet-case," "a portentous wooden snuff-gourd," "horn-rimmed spectacles of Chinese work." This accumulation of precise material detail is what creates Kipling's famous sense of immersion -- you can *smell* this world because he has named its specific odors (artemisia, deodar, wood-smoke, jasmine, marigold, dust).
- **Vocabulary range:** Very wide, drawing from at least four linguistic reservoirs: formal English (imperial, administrative, military), colloquial Anglo-Indian English ("up-country," "down-country," "bazar-talk"), transliterated Indian languages (dozens of terms per chapter), and archaic/Biblical English in the lama's speech ("Whither goest thou?", "Thinkest thou?", "Hast thou eaten?"). Kipling moves between these registers without friction, and the range itself enacts the novel's central theme of cultural multiplicity.
- **Signature vocabulary:** "Trove," "intrigue," "provenance," "commission," "device," "acquaintance," "prudence." Also the recurrent Anglo-Indian vocabulary: "_serai_" (caravansary), "_sahib_" (master/sir), "_sirkar_" (government), "_rissaldar_" (cavalry officer), "_pice_/_anna_/_rupee_" (currency), "_te-rain_" (train, phonetically rendered). The word "game" recurs with special force -- Kim's life is a "game," espionage is a "game," begging is a "game." "The Great Game" is never named in these chapters but its conceptual shadow falls across every page.
- **Avoids:** Abstract emotional vocabulary. Characters are never "sad," "happy," or "anxious" in narration -- they are shown in action, through gesture and speech. Extended interior monologue. Stream-of-consciousness. Philosophical abstraction in the narrative voice (though the lama speaks philosophically, the narrator does not). Hedging or uncertainty -- Kipling's narrator is always confident, always authoritative, sometimes to the point of didacticism.

### Tone
- **Emotional temperature:** Warm but unsentimental. The default temperature is that of a man sitting by a campfire telling a story he loves to people he trusts -- engaged, vivid, occasionally amused, but never mawkish. The warmest moments are rendered through action rather than commentary: Kim flinging a meal at the lama, the old soldier saluting, the lama trailing his rosary for the child. The narrator's affection for his characters is palpable but controlled. When genuine feeling threatens to surface -- the lama's loneliness, Kim's motherlessness, the old soldier's fading strength -- Kipling deflects with a terse sentence or a scene-shift. Sentimentality is the one thing this prose will not permit.
- **Narrative distance:** Close but mobile. The narrator occupies a position of intimate knowledge -- he knows what people think, what they eat, how they smell, why they act as they do -- but he does not fully enter anyone's consciousness. Instead, he moves among characters like an expert guide, pausing to explain a custom, translate a phrase, or deliver a judgment. He is closest to Kim, whose perceptions drive most of the action, but even Kim's thoughts are rendered externally: "Kim warmed to the game" rather than the game's interior experience. The narrator is further from the lama, who remains somewhat mysterious, and furthest from the English officials, who appear as types. This variable distance is managed through a consistent authorial voice that is itself a character -- knowing, masculine, worldly, and faintly amused.
- **Humor:** Broad, situational, and character-driven. Three primary mechanisms:
  1. **The street-smart con:** Kim's manipulations of adults are funny because the reader is in on the joke while the marks are not. His performance for the old woman -- flattery, false modesty, strategic insolence -- is comedy because we see the twelve-year-old's brain calculating behind the beggar's tears.
  2. **Cultural collision:** The lama in the train, the farmer confronted by his own superstition, the Amritzar girl's ambiguous charity -- humor arises from the friction between different worldviews occupying the same physical space. Kipling does not mock any single culture; he finds the comedy in the gaps between all of them.
  3. **The anthropological punchline:** The narrator delivers a perfectly deadpan observation that is simultaneously a joke and a truth: "India is the only democratic land in the world." "Dynamite was milky and innocuous beside that report of C25." These land with comic force because of their tonal flatness -- the narrator does not signal that he is being funny.
- **Conflict rendering:** Direct, physical, and verbal. Conflict is rendered through confrontation, not subtext. Characters shout, insult, threaten, whip, and curse. Even sophisticated diplomatic maneuvering (Mahbub Ali's espionage) is described through concrete physical action -- the search of the stall, the folded tissue-paper, the clink of a rupee. Verbal combat is elaborate, inventive, and culturally specific: the old woman's invective is described as a marvel of "blistering, biting appropriateness." Violence, when it appears, is matter-of-fact: "two or three family blood-feuds across the Border hung unfinished on his hands."

### Imagery
- **Sensory emphasis:** Overwhelmingly visual and olfactory, with strong tactile and auditory elements. Kipling's world is experienced through the body. He describes heat, dust, smoke, the smell of artemisia and marigold and dung-fires, the feel of a goat-skin water bag, the sound of well-wheels and train brakes and creaking cart-axles. Color is used frequently and specifically: "golden, rose, saffron, and pink, the morning mists smoked away across the flat green levels"; "a yellow-and-brown streak" (the cobra); "Isabella-coloured clothes"; the "mouse-coloured Brahmini bull." Taste appears through food: "hot rice," "fried cake," "tamarind conserve," "clarified butter." This is a SIGNATURE sensory richness -- the world is built from named, concrete physical details, never from abstractions.
- **Metaphor frequency:** Moderate. Kipling uses metaphor selectively but vividly, and his figures tend toward the concrete and physical rather than the literary or philosophical. When he reaches for a figure, it is often drawn from the natural or working world: the lama "expanded like the Bodhisat's own lotus"; a child "toppled over sideways" like a heavy fruit; the lama "slackened off, joint by joint, like a slow camel"; the crowd was "a vast improvement on the cramped and crowded Lahore streets." The figures are quick, visual, and often comic in their precision.
- **Metaphor style:** When present, drawn from the physical and animal world: camels, horses, crows, tortoises, bats, caterpillars. The Grand Trunk Road itself becomes the central sustained metaphor: "a river of life as nowhere else exists in the world." Social relations are persistently rendered through spatial and kinetic metaphor -- people "drift," "shuffle," "stalk," "stroll," "loom," "shrink." Movement verbs carry enormous weight; a character's gait is their moral signature. The lama "strides" and "shuffles"; Kim "darts," "dodges," "slips," and "dances"; Mahbub Ali "rolls" and "strolls." These are not decorative -- they are the primary mechanism of characterization.
- **Physical grounding:** Total and obsessive. Every scene is grounded in a specific, named place with specific physical features: the brick platform of Zam-Zammah, the wooden cubicle of the Curator's office, the heat-split cedar door with its knot-hole, the masonry platforms of the railway station, the arched cloisters of the Kashmir Serai. Landscapes are rendered with cartographic precision: distances in miles and _kos_, directions on the compass, the position of the Himalayas relative to the road. Rooms are described by their contents -- packing-cases, saddle-bags, hookah, silk carpets. The built environment communicates social information: the serai's locked doors with their chalk inscriptions, the police-station at the _parao_, the bullock-cart with its broidered double-domed canopy signaling a person of rank.

### Rhythm
- **Pacing:** Varied and deliberate, driven by alternation between three modes: (1) sweeping panoramic description that moves at the speed of a walking eye, accumulating detail; (2) rapid-fire dialogue exchanges that accelerate the pace dramatically; and (3) compressed narrative summary that collapses hours, days, or years into a few sentences. The transitions between these modes are often abrupt -- a page of bustling description will cut to a two-line dialogue exchange, then to a single sentence of summary: "And at last -- tired, sleepy, and dusty -- they reached Umballa City Station." This abruptness is SIGNATURE. Kipling does not ease his reader between modes; he cuts, and the reader adjusts.
- **Paragraph length:** Highly variable. Descriptive panoramas run long (10-20 sentences), building through accumulated parallel structures. Dialogue scenes break into short paragraphs of 1-3 sentences, each paragraph a single speech and tag. Narrative summary paragraphs are mid-length (4-8 sentences). The longest paragraphs are the anthropological excursuses -- the passages where the narrator explains a custom, a history, or a social structure. These function as set-pieces and can run 15+ sentences.
- **Dialogue balance:** Dialogue constitutes roughly 40-50% of the text in Chapters I and IV, closer to 30% in Chapters II and III (which contain more landscape and crowd description). Within dialogue scenes, Kipling gives more speaking time to characters with more social fluency -- Kim speaks most, the lama next, other characters in proportion to their narrative weight. Crucially, dialogue in Kipling is always *rendered* -- given in direct speech with specific verbal tics and idiom -- never merely reported. Each speaker has their own syntax, their own vocabulary, their own rhythm. The lama uses archaic interrogatives ("Thinkest thou?", "Hast thou eaten?"), Kim uses street-smart declaratives and imperatives ("Give me the bowl," "Come!"), Mahbub Ali uses blunt, gruff commands ("Go! Little Hindu, run away"). This individuated dialogue is a SIGNATURE achievement.
- **Punctuation signature:** Heavy use of em-dashes for three distinct purposes: (1) narrative interruption and parenthetical insertion ("the Ajaib-Gher -- the Wonder House, as the natives call the Lahore Museum"); (2) dramatic pause within dialogue ("I came by Kulu -- from beyond the Kailas -- but what know you?"); (3) translation and glossing of vernacular terms ("a _chela_ (disciple)"). Semicolons for cataloguing and list-building within long descriptive sentences. Exclamation marks in dialogue, sometimes doubled, for the urgent, declamatory quality of Indian speech patterns ("Let me up!" "Off! Off!"). Italics for all Indian-language terms, which appear on nearly every page and give the text its distinctive visual texture. The em-dash is SIGNATURE: it is Kipling's instrument of layering, allowing two linguistic or cultural registers to coexist within a single sentence.

## Voice Notes

What makes Kipling unmistakable is the **double authority** of the narrative voice. At any moment, the prose is doing two things at once: telling a vivid, kinetic, forward-moving story AND delivering an ethnographic education. The narrator knows this world -- its castes, its customs, its languages, its smells, its geography, its politics -- and he cannot help teaching you about it even as he entertains you. This is not pedantry; it is passion. The information is woven so tightly into the narrative texture that removing it would collapse the story. When Kipling tells you that "India is the only democratic land in the world" as Chota Lal's father is worth half a million sterling, the sociological observation IS the narrative detail. They are the same thing.

The skilled mimic would focus first on this: **every scene is simultaneously a story and a briefing.** The overt action -- Kim begs food, the lama searches for a river, Mahbub Ali passes intelligence -- is always embedded in a matrix of cultural information delivered with the confidence of a man who has lived it. To write in Kipling's voice, you must know (or convincingly invent) the specific, material details of your world: the caste of the vegetable seller, the breed of the horse, the denomination of the coin, the name of the gate. Vagueness is the one thing this prose cannot tolerate.

Second: **the linguistic layering.** Kipling's English does not merely describe a multilingual world; it *performs* multilingualism. Indian terms in italics, parenthetical translations, phonetic renderings (_te-rain_ for train), archaic English standing in for formal Urdu or Tibetan, street slang rendered as street slang -- the text is a palimpsest of languages. Every character speaks in a way that locates them precisely in the social and cultural grid. Kim code-switches between registers; the lama speaks in a register that suggests ancient scripture translated into English; Mahbub Ali speaks with the rough directness of a man who bargains for a living. To achieve this effect, the writer must create a consistent system of linguistic markers for each culture and each character, and deploy them without explanation when context makes the meaning clear.

Third: **the refusal of interiority.** Kipling almost never tells you what a character feels. He shows you what they do, what they say, how they move. The lama's loneliness is rendered as "he stared dizzily in all directions" and "wailed." Kim's excitement is "his eyes aflame" and "his eyes danced in his head." The old soldier's pride is his posture -- "sitting as erect as the younger." This externalized psychology is what gives the prose its speed and forward drive. Feelings are not explored; they are *enacted*.

Fourth: **the panoramic set-piece.** Kipling regularly pauses the narrative for extended descriptive passages that render a scene as a teeming, crowded, many-layered spectacle -- the Kashmir Serai, the Grand Trunk Road, the _parao_ at sunset. These passages are built through accumulated parallel structures (present participles, semicoloned clauses) that create a sense of overwhelming variety and simultaneity. They are the prose equivalent of a wide-angle tracking shot, and they are designed to immerse the reader in the sensory totality of a place. The trick is specificity: every item in the catalogue is named, located, and culturally identified. "A solid line of blue, rising and falling like the back of a caterpillar in haste" is not generic humanity -- it is a specific caste of female road-workers, wearing specific blue petticoats, moving in a specific way.

Fifth: **the unsentimental tenderness.** Kipling loves his characters -- the lama's innocence, Kim's audacity, the old soldier's pride, the old woman's ferocity -- but he will not say so. Affection is rendered through the quality of attention: the narrative lingers on the lama's "thousand-wrinkled face" and his "most simple triumph," on Kim's "clear yell" and his "bare heels." When the lama says to Kim, "to none among men, if so be thou art woman-born, has my heart gone out as it has to thee -- thoughtful, wise, and courteous; but something of a small imp," the tenderness is in the characters' voices, not the narrator's. The narrator merely reports, with an implied warmth that never becomes explicit.

## Quick Reference

When writing in this voice:
- **Lead with material specificity, not abstraction.** Name the caste, the coin, the breed, the gate, the fabric, the spice. A room is not "cluttered" -- it has "silk carpet saddle-bags," "an immense silver hookah," and "a deep, embroidered Bokhariot belt." Every noun earns its place by being exact.
- **Layer languages within the prose.** Use italicized foreign terms with parenthetical glosses on first appearance, then without translation thereafter. Let characters speak in syntactic patterns that reflect their cultural origin. Create a phonetic rendering for any technology or concept being encountered for the first time by a character unfamiliar with it.
- **Build panoramic passages through accumulated parallel structures.** Use present participles and semicolons to create long catalogue sentences that sweep across a scene: "loading and unloading; drawing water; piling grass; cuffing dogs; swearing, shouting, arguing." Each item should add a new sensory or social detail.
- **Keep dialogue short, vivid, and character-specific.** Each speaker must have identifiable verbal habits. Use archaic English (thee/thou, inverted syntax) for formal or devotional speech. Use clipped imperatives for street-smart characters. Use gruff, blunt sentences for military or merchant characters. Never let two characters sound alike.
- **Deliver cultural information with total confidence.** The narrator is an authority. He does not hedge, qualify, or wonder. He *knows.* "Ticket-collecting is a slow business in the East." "All India is full of holy men stammering gospels in strange tongues." These generalizations are delivered as facts, with the assurance of long residence. The confidence is the register.
- **Render emotion through action and gesture, not through naming feelings.** A character does not "feel sad" -- he "bowed his head on his knees and wailed." A character is not "excited" -- "his eyes danced in his head." Translate every interior state into a visible, physical act.
- **Use the epigrammatic snap for emphasis.** After a longer passage, drop a single short declarative sentence that reframes or punctuates everything that preceded it. "Kim's mother had been Irish, too." "That was perfectly true." "Dynamite was milky and innocuous beside that report." These sentences should be dry, flat, and devastating in their compression.
- **Let humor arise from cultural collision and street-level cleverness.** Comedy comes from the friction between different worldviews sharing the same space, and from characters outsmarting their situations through wit and nerve. Do not write jokes; write characters being themselves in a world that is more various than any single perspective can contain.

## Sample Passage

The boy found Anwar sitting cross-legged on his string-bed in the shadow of the neem tree, peeling a blood-orange with a _kard_ whose blade had been ground down to a fingerbreadth through forty years of such service. Anwar's people -- Pathan traders from the Khyber side, who had settled two generations back and now sold carpets to the cantonment wives -- did not peel fruit. They bit and tore. But Anwar had served the English, first as a _syce_ in a cavalry mess and later as something less easy to name, and he had acquired certain of their habits along with certain of their secrets.

"You are late," he said, not looking up. The orange peel fell in one long unbroken coil.

"The Doab road was thick with cane-carts. Nanda Singh's ox threw a shoe at the bridge and all Doab stood still for it."

"And what does Doab say of the new _Doputty_?"

"They say he is young. They say he has a yellow moustache and rides a mare that is too good for him. They say he asks questions."

"Ah." Anwar quartered the orange and offered half without ceremony. The juice ran between the boy's fingers and dripped on the packed earth. Three ants found it at once and began their frantic commerce. "And does he ask of me?"

"Of you, of Doost Khan, of the woman at the silk-house, of the boatmen below the barrage. He asks of all men." The boy wiped his chin. "He has a book."

"They always have a book," said Anwar. He leaned back against the trunk of the neem and closed his eyes. Somewhere in the bazaar a coppersmith was beating out a _lota_, the even blows ringing clear above the midday drone of flies and the faint, far whistle of the 2:40 mail heading south toward Doab Junction.

"I know books," Anwar continued. "I have carried books from Peshawur to Doab and from Doab to places where books are not welcome. A book is nothing." He opened one eye. "It is the man who reads the book."

"Then this man reads well," said the boy. "The Doputty was three hours with the _patwari_ this morning, and the _patwari_ came out sweating."

"Three hours! But it is hot. Even an honest man sweats in this weather."

"The _patwari_ is not an honest man."

"No," said Anwar, and for the first time he smiled -- a slow, thin, appreciative smile that never reached higher than his moustache. "No, he is not. That, at least, the new Doputty has discovered. It is a beginning."
